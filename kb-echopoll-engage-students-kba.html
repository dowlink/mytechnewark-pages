Using EchoPoll to Increase Student Engagement

Best Practice Engage Your Students RU-N & RBS Only EchoPoll · Canvas

EchoPoll is Rutgers University–Newark's classroom engagement platform, integrated directly with Canvas. When used intentionally, EchoPoll transforms passive lectures into active learning experiences — giving every student a voice, generating real-time insight, and creating the conditions for deeper discussion.

⚑ RU-N & RBS Faculty Only EchoPoll is licensed exclusively for Rutgers University–Newark and Rutgers Business School faculty. This tool is not available at other Rutgers campuses. Contact ATS at atshelp@newark.rutgers.edu if you have questions about access.

Overview

EchoPoll allows instructors to create interactive learning experiences before, during, and after class. When used intentionally, EchoPoll can:

  • Increase participation across the full class
  • Encourage active learning
  • Check student understanding in real time
  • Foster discussion and critical thinking
  • Build classroom community
  • Provide immediate feedback for instruction

EchoPoll works in face-to-face, hybrid, and online courses and is integrated directly with Canvas.

When to Use EchoPoll

EchoPoll works particularly well when you want students to:

  • Participate during lectures
  • Share ideas anonymously
  • Check understanding of key concepts
  • Reflect on course content
  • Discuss complex or controversial topics
  • Engage in active learning activities

EchoPoll is most effective when used throughout a class session rather than only at the beginning or end.

Why Polling Improves Engagement

Traditional lectures often rely on a small number of students to answer questions. EchoPoll allows every student to participate simultaneously — making engagement the default, not the exception.

Full-Class Participation

Every student responds simultaneously, not just those who volunteer.

More Inclusive Classrooms

Anonymous polling ensures every voice is heard, including quieter students.

Real-Time Feedback

Immediate results let you adjust instruction on the fly based on what students actually understand.

Improved Retention

Active participation throughout the session helps students stay attentive and retain course concepts.

Anonymous participation also encourages contributions from students who may be reluctant to speak publicly — particularly on sensitive topics or when they are uncertain of the correct answer.

Best Practices for Student Engagement

🎯
Start with a Purpose

Every poll should support a teaching objective — checking understanding, generating discussion, gathering opinions, encouraging reflection. Avoid polling simply because the technology is available.

🔁
Poll Frequently

Short interactions throughout class are more effective than a single poll at the end. Add activities at the beginning, after major concepts, before transitions, and at the end. Frequent engagement helps maintain attention.

💬
Use Poll Results to Drive Discussion

Polling should start conversations, not end them. After displaying results, ask students to explain their reasoning, compare perspectives, or address misconceptions. The discussion after the poll is often where the deepest learning occurs.

🤝
Encourage Anonymous Participation

Anonymous activities increase participation and provide more honest responses. Use for opinion questions, sensitive topics, self-assessment, and reflection. Students contribute more freely when they are not concerned about being wrong.

⏱️
Keep Activities Short

Most engagement activities should take only a few minutes. Effective activities often include one question, one discussion, and one key takeaway. Short activities maintain momentum while keeping students engaged.

Engagement Activity Types

These activity types work well across disciplines and course modalities:

👋 Icebreakers

Learn about your students and build community. Examples: What is your major? What experience do you have with this topic?

Best for: First day of class
❓ Knowledge Checks

Ask students to apply concepts immediately after instruction using concept questions, scenario-based questions, or prediction questions.

Best for: Formative assessment and identifying misconceptions
☁️ Word Clouds

Collect short responses and display common themes visually. Examples: What word best describes today's topic? What concept remains unclear?

Best for: Reflection and brainstorming
⚖️ Opinion Polls

Present a question with multiple viewpoints to spark discussion. Examples: Which approach is most effective? What would you do in this situation?

Best for: Discussion and critical thinking
🎟️ Exit Tickets

Gather feedback before students leave class. Examples: What was your biggest takeaway? What remains unclear? What should we revisit?

Best for: Reflection and instructional planning

Sample Classroom Workflows

Integrate EchoPoll at any point in a class session:

Before Class
  • Survey prior knowledge
  • Review assigned readings
  • Collect student questions
During Class
  • Check understanding after key concepts
  • Run opinion or prediction polls
  • Facilitate discussion from results
  • Monitor engagement with reflection prompts
After Class
  • Gather feedback on pacing and clarity
  • Reinforce key concepts with an exit ticket
  • Encourage reflection on what students will apply

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Too Many Questions

Polling should support instruction, not overwhelm students. Focus on quality over quantity — one or two well-chosen questions per class session is often more effective than six.

⚠️ Not Discussing Results

Avoid moving on immediately after students respond. Use responses as a teaching opportunity — especially when the class is split or when a common misconception surfaces.

⚠️ Using Only Factual Questions

Mix factual, analytical, and opinion-based questions. Discussion questions and open-ended prompts often generate the highest engagement and the richest class conversations.

⚠️ Waiting Until Students Are Disengaged

Polling works best as a regular instructional strategy woven into each class session, not as a last-minute attempt to regain attention.

ATS Recommendation

💡 Start with One Activity Per Class Session Good first activities include a knowledge check, a word cloud, an opinion poll, or an exit ticket. As you become comfortable with EchoPoll, expand to multiple engagement points throughout the lesson.

Remember: the goal is not simply to collect responses. The goal is to create opportunities for students to think, participate, and interact with course content.
