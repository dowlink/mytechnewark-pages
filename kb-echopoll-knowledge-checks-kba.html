Using EchoPoll for Knowledge Checks and Formative Assessment

Best Practice Engage Your Students RU-N & RBS Only EchoPoll · Canvas

EchoPoll can be used to assess student understanding throughout a class session using low-stakes formative assessment activities. By collecting responses from every student, EchoPoll helps instructors identify misconceptions, monitor understanding, and adjust instruction in real time.

⚑ RU-N & RBS Faculty Only EchoPoll is licensed exclusively for Rutgers University–Newark and Rutgers Business School faculty. This tool is not available at other Rutgers campuses. Contact ATS at atshelp@newark.rutgers.edu if you have questions about access.

Overview

Unlike exams and graded assignments, formative assessments are designed to provide immediate feedback to both students and instructors. Knowledge checks can help instructors:

  • Identify misconceptions before they become learning barriers
  • Monitor student understanding during class
  • Adjust instruction in real time
  • Increase participation and engagement
  • Reinforce key concepts and improve learning outcomes

What Is Formative Assessment?

Formative assessment is the process of gathering information about student learning while instruction is taking place. The goal is not to assign grades — it is to help instructors and students understand where learning is and where it needs to go.

Formative assessment helps instructors answer questions such as:

  • Do students understand this concept?
  • Are there misconceptions that need to be addressed?
  • Is the class ready to move on?
  • Should additional examples or practice be provided?

EchoPoll provides immediate insight into student understanding, allowing instructors to make instructional decisions based on real-time feedback rather than assumptions.

When to Use Knowledge Checks

Knowledge checks can be used at any point in a class session:

🌅 Before Instruction
"How familiar are you with today's topic?"
Best for: activating prior knowledge and preparing students for new material
🔍 During Instruction
"Which explanation best describes the relationship we just discussed?"
Best for: identifying misconceptions and deciding whether to move on
📖 After a Major Concept
"Which concept remains unclear?"
Best for: determining whether additional review or examples are needed
🎟️ End of Class
"What was the most important thing you learned today?"
Best for: reflection, retention, and instructional planning

Best Practices

🧠
Ask Questions That Require Thinking

Avoid relying exclusively on recall or memorization. Instead of asking "What year did this event occur?", ask "Why was this event significant?" Questions that require analysis, interpretation, or application generate deeper learning and more meaningful discussion.

🤔
Poll Before Revealing the Answer

Allow students to commit to an answer before reviewing the correct response. This increases engagement, improves retention, encourages more honest responses, and builds critical thinking. Students are more likely to remember concepts when they actively reason through a question before receiving feedback.

📊
Use Results to Guide Instruction

The value of polling comes from what happens after students respond. Review common misconceptions, ask students to explain their reasoning, compare different perspectives, and revisit difficult concepts. Knowledge checks should inform teaching decisions, not simply collect data.

👥
Encourage Peer Discussion

When responses are mixed, use the results as a structured discussion opportunity. This approach — sometimes called Peer Instruction — often leads to meaningful gains in understanding in just a few minutes:

Peer Instruction Sequence
  1. 1Ask the question — display it to the class
  2. 2Display the results — show the distribution without revealing the correct answer
  3. 3Have students discuss — pairs or small groups explain their reasoning
  4. 4Poll again — re-run the same question to see whether understanding has shifted
  5. 5Review together — discuss the correct answer and explore why responses differed
🔓
Keep Activities Low Stakes

Students should feel comfortable making mistakes. Knowledge checks work best when participation is encouraged, responses are anonymous or low pressure, and mistakes are treated as learning opportunities. The goal is to improve learning, not evaluate performance.

Activity Ideas

✅ Concept Check

Ask students to apply a concept immediately after instruction.

Best for: identifying misconceptions in real time
🌍 Scenario-Based Question

Present a real-world situation and ask students to apply course concepts.

Best for: critical thinking and application
🔮 Prediction Question

Ask students to predict an outcome before revealing the answer.

Best for: building curiosity and engagement
💯 Confidence Poll

Ask: How confident are you in your understanding of today's material? (Very / Somewhat / Not Yet Confident)

Best for: identifying areas that need additional review
💬 Muddiest Point

Ask: What concept remains most confusing?

Best for: gathering feedback and planning future instruction
🎟️ Exit Ticket

Ask: What is one thing you learned today?

Best for: reflection and reinforcement at the end of class

Sample Classroom Workflow

This six-step sequence integrates a knowledge check naturally into any lesson:

  1. 1
    Introduce a concept — present new content, a demonstration, or an example.
  2. 2
    Launch an EchoPoll question — ask students to apply or interpret what they have just learned.
  3. 3
    Review responses — display results and identify patterns. Where is the class aligned? Where is there disagreement?
  4. 4
    Discuss the results — explore student reasoning and address misconceptions. Call on students to explain their thinking.
  5. 5
    Clarify concepts — provide additional explanation or examples where the results indicate confusion.
  6. 6
    Continue instruction — move forward once students demonstrate understanding.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Treating Polls Like Tests

Knowledge checks should support learning rather than create anxiety. Avoid grading every activity. When students fear being wrong, they disengage — the opposite of what formative assessment is designed to achieve.

⚠️ Ignoring the Results

Students quickly recognize when polling activities are not being used meaningfully. Review and discuss results whenever possible. Even a 60-second debrief after a poll signals that their responses matter.

⚠️ Asking Only Recall Questions

Mix factual, conceptual, and application-based questions. The strongest discussions often emerge from questions that require reasoning — not questions with a single correct answer.

⚠️ Moving Too Quickly

Give students adequate time to think before responding — at least 30 to 60 seconds for most questions. Allow time for discussion after reviewing results. Rushing undermines the learning value of the activity.

ATS Recommendation

💡 Build Regular Moments of Reflection into Every Class A simple knowledge check every 10–15 minutes can help maintain attention, increase participation, identify misconceptions, and improve learning outcomes. The most effective instructors use polling not to evaluate students, but to understand what students are thinking — and to teach more responsively as a result.
