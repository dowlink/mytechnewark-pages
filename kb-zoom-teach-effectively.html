Teach Effectively in Zoom

Practical guidance for RU-N faculty on running productive Zoom class sessions — from opening routines and engagement tools to recording, post-session follow-up in Canvas, and troubleshooting when things go wrong.

Category:  Teach in the Classroom
Audience:  RU-N Faculty
Last reviewed:  May 2026
Reviewed by:  TLS, Rutgers–Newark

Starting the Session Well

Join your Zoom session a few minutes before the scheduled start time. This gives students a chance to connect, test their audio, and resolve technical issues before instruction begins — and it reduces the dead time at the start of class that disrupts momentum.

A short, consistent opening routine helps students transition into the learning environment. Simple options that work well in Zoom:

  • A welcome slide with the session topic and agenda visible as students join
  • A brief verbal or chat-based attendance prompt
  • A warm-up discussion question posted in the chat for students to respond to while latecomers join
  • A 30-second preview of what the class will cover and what students should have ready
💡 Consistency matters
The same opening routine every session reduces cognitive load — students know what to expect when they join and can settle in without waiting for instructions. Even a simple "Welcome slide + agenda" posted before class is enough to signal that the session is structured and intentional.

Engagement Tools During Class

Zoom's built-in tools can vary interaction methods throughout a session, which helps maintain attention and gives different types of learners ways to participate. Use them intentionally rather than all at once.

🖥️
Screen Sharing Share slides, live websites, documents, or demonstrations directly in the session. Pause periodically when screen sharing to check chat — students often type questions while you're presenting rather than unmuting.
💬
Chat The chat panel lets students ask questions, respond to prompts, and participate without interrupting. Ask specific chat questions ("Type your answer in the chat — don't send until I say go") to generate simultaneous responses that spark discussion.
📊
Polls Use polls for quick comprehension checks, informal attendance, or anonymous feedback. Launch a poll mid-lecture to break up a long presentation and gauge where students are before moving on.
👥
Breakout Rooms Small-group discussion and collaborative activities. Before opening rooms, clearly state the discussion topic, the time limit, and what students should produce or report back. Unstructured breakout rooms produce weak results — clear expectations make the difference.
✋
Reactions and Hand Raise Students can signal agreement, confusion, or a desire to speak without unmuting. Build these into your session ("Give me a thumbs up if this makes sense" or "Raise your hand if you want to respond") so students know the option exists.

Communicate Expectations to Students

Students participate more confidently when they know what is expected. Before the first Zoom session of the semester, communicate clearly how Zoom will be used in your course. Students should know:

  • When and how to join each session — the meeting link, whether it recurs, and where to find it
  • Whether attendance is required and how it will be recorded
  • Your expectations for camera and microphone use
  • How to ask questions during class — chat, hand raise, or unmuting
  • What to do if they lose connection or experience a technical problem mid-session
📋
Post a Zoom Participation Guide in Canvas
Consider adding a short Zoom participation guide to your Canvas course — in the syllabus module or the first week's module — so students can review expectations at any time. It doesn't need to be long: a bulleted list covering the join link, attendance policy, camera/mic expectations, and how to ask questions is enough. Students who know the rules in advance ask fewer procedural questions during class.

Recording and Post-Session Follow-Up

If you plan to record your Zoom sessions, inform students before recording begins — verbally at the start of class and ideally in the course syllabus or participation guide as well.

  1. Review the recording if needed — After the session ends, Zoom processes and saves the recording. Review it briefly if students have reported audio or technical issues before sharing.
  2. Share the recording with the appropriate audience only — Share the recording link with enrolled students via Canvas — not via a public link or email to an unconfirmed list. For recordings containing student participation, use a restricted sharing link.
  3. Upload supporting materials to Canvas — After each session, post the recording link alongside any slides, notes, follow-up activities, discussion prompts, or quizzes in the relevant Canvas module.
🎓
Keep Recordings and Materials Together in Canvas
Whenever possible, place the recording link and all related materials — slides, notes, follow-up assignments — together in the same Canvas module for that week or session. Students who miss a class or want to review should be able to continue the lesson without searching across multiple Canvas areas. A module that contains the recording, the slides, and a reflection prompt is a complete learning experience; a recording link posted as an announcement is not.

Troubleshooting During a Session

When something goes wrong in a live Zoom session, start with the simplest fixes first before escalating.

  • Audio not working — Confirm the correct microphone and speaker are selected in Zoom's audio settings (^ next to the Mute button → Audio Settings). Ask the affected participant to leave and rejoin the meeting.
  • Camera not showing — Check that another application (Teams, FaceTime, a browser tab) isn't already using the camera. Ask the participant to stop video and restart it, or leave and rejoin.
  • Screen share freezes or stops — Stop sharing and restart it. If the issue persists, restart Zoom. Share slides as a PDF in the chat as a backup so students can follow along while you troubleshoot.
  • Your own audio/video drops mid-session — Restart Zoom rather than the entire computer if possible. Use Canvas Announcements or a posted chat message to let students know you're reconnecting.
  • A student can't hear or see anything — Have them leave the meeting completely and rejoin from a fresh browser tab or the Zoom desktop app.
📣
Use Canvas When Zoom Is Down
If you cannot resolve a Zoom issue mid-session, use a Canvas Announcement to communicate with students immediately — post a brief note explaining the situation, any alternative instructions, and when you expect to reconnect or whether the session will be rescheduled. Students who have already left the Zoom session can still receive Canvas announcements via email notification.

Reporting Recurring Issues

For problems that persist across multiple sessions or that you cannot resolve yourself, contact the RU-N IT Service Center and include the following information to speed up resolution:

  • Course name and section number
  • Date and time of the affected session
  • A brief description of the problem and what you tried
  • Any error messages received — screenshots are helpful

Do's and Don'ts

✓ Do
  • Join a few minutes early to catch technical issues before class starts
  • Use a consistent opening routine so students know what to expect
  • Vary interaction methods — chat, polls, reactions, breakout rooms
  • Give clear instructions before opening breakout rooms
  • Inform students before recording begins
  • Post recordings and materials together in the same Canvas module
  • Communicate Zoom expectations clearly at the start of the semester
✕ Don't
  • Start class at exactly the scheduled time — join early
  • Open breakout rooms without explaining the task and time limit first
  • Share recording links publicly or with anyone outside the course
  • Leave students waiting without communication if Zoom goes down
  • Post the recording alone without the supporting materials students need
  • Assume students know how to use Zoom participation features without being shown
